ü™ú The Value Ladder (Daniel Priestley Framework)

Every successful business has at least 3 product tiers. You should LOSE MONEY or break-even on tiers 1-2 and PROFIT on tier 3+.

Tier 1: Free or Low Cost

$0 - $10

Goal: Establish commitment & consistency. Get them in the door.

Examples: Free masterclass, $1 webinar, Lead magnet, Free trial

Tier 2: Standard Price Product

$100 - $500

Goal: Break-even. Cover marketing costs. Build trust.

Examples: Course, Membership, Workshop, Book + course bundle

Tier 3: High-Value Offering

$1,000 - $10,000

Goal: PROFIT. This is where you make money.

Examples: Mastermind, Certification, Done-for-you service, 1-on-1 coaching

Tier 4: Premium/Recurring

$10,000+

Goal: Long-term retention. Maximum lifetime value.

Examples: Partnership, Luxury experiences (A-Fest), Ongoing consulting

‚ö° The 3 Types of Upsells

  1. BETTER Upsell

    What it is: Enhance the core product with premium features or additions.

    McDonald's Example: "Add fries to make your meal complete."

    Your Business: Core course ($299) ‚Üí Add live coaching calls ($200)

  2. FASTER Upsell

    What it is: Priority processing, expedited service, jump the queue.

    Example: Peter Diamandis charges $500 to get your Abundance 360 application processed faster.

    Your Business: Standard application ‚Üí Fast-track review ($500)

  3. MORE Upsell

    What it is: Bundle multiple products together at a discount.

    Mindvalley Example: Entrepreneurship Mastery ($5,000) + AI Mastery (normally $6,000) = Get both for $8,000 (save $3,000)

    Your Business: One course ($499) ‚Üí All 4 courses bundle ($1,497, save $500)

Anatomy of a Perfect Upsell Page:

‚ùì Student Q&A Highlights

How many upsells are okay before the client feels like you only want their money?

Answer: Limit to 2 upsells maximum. So you're selling 3 products total: Core product + Upsell 1 + Upsell 2.

Some companies go to 3 upsells, but it depends on how you phrase it. The key is that each upsell must genuinely ADD VALUE and feel helpful, not pushy.

Can upsells work for physical products?

Answer: Absolutely! McDonald's fries are physical products. Upsells STARTED with physical products.

Examples: "Customers who bought this also bought..." on Amazon, "Add batteries for $5" when buying electronics, "Want fries with that?"

How do I test which upsell offers work best?

Answer: Dedicate a testing budget (e.g., $1,000). Run your funnel for 1-2 weeks and measure:

  • AOV (Average Order Value) ‚Äî Did it go up?
  • ROAS (Return on Ad Spend) ‚Äî Are you profitable?
  • Conversion Rate ‚Äî Are people still buying the core product?

Then tweak one variable: pricing, copy, offer structure, placement. Test again. Repeat.

Should I send emails weekly, bi-weekly, or monthly?

Answer: Once per week is the sweet spot for most audiences. You can test twice per week, but monitor unsubscribe rates closely.

Key: Keep emails valuable. Share stories, tips, insights ‚Äî not just sales pitches. Many Mindvalley authors email weekly and see excellent engagement.

How do I know WHO to upsell to?

Answer: Target customers with HIGH ENGAGEMENT:

  • Using your product/service actively
  • Reached a milestone (completed course, attended 3 events)
  • Active in your community (commenting, sharing)
  • Return users (logged in multiple times)

These users are most likely to buy again because they're already seeing value.

What if I'm selling on Amazon? Can I still do upsells?

Answer: Yes, but your upsell has to be IN THE BOOK/PRODUCT itself.

In the front or back of the book, include: "If you enjoyed this book, visit [YourWebsite.com] to learn about my $400 course or book a consultation."

Amazon won't give you the customer's email, so you must direct them off-platform.

‚ùå Critical DON'Ts

  • Don't upsell before delivering value. Let them experience the core product first.
  • Don't do too many upsells. Limit to 2 maximum.
  • Don't suggest unrelated products. Upsells must be logical next steps.
  • Don't make checkout complicated. One-click is key.
  • Don't ignore the customer journey stage. Acknowledge what they just bought.
