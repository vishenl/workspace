‚ú® 10 Things You Should Know as a Teacher

Here's what actually works, straight from your students. Read this with your coffee and a highlighter. ‚òï

  1. Use Clear, Structured, Actionable Teaching

    Listen, nobody likes wandering through a maze when they signed up for a guided tour. Give your students frameworks, sequences, templates, checklists, and "do-this-next" instructions. 92-99% of students across every single program said this was the #1 thing that helped them succeed. Structure isn't boring‚Äîit's kindness. It's how you show respect for people's time and trust.

    Non-Negotiable

  2. Bring Inspiring Energy & Emotional Resonance

    You're not a robot reading PowerPoint slides (please tell me you're not). Students want to FEEL something. They want to leave your class feeling more confident, more hopeful, more capable than when they arrived. 68-91% said this was essential. Be real. Be passionate. Show them why this stuff matters. Your enthusiasm is contagious‚Äîor your lack of it is, too. Choose wisely. üòâ

    Essential Magic

  3. Provide Practical Tools & Templates They Can Actually Use

    Theory is great. You know what's better? Giving students something they can download, open, and use tomorrow morning. Prompts. Scripts. Slide decks. Exercises. Worksheets. The stuff that makes them think, "Holy crap, I can actually DO this now." When you hand over the goods, ratings skyrocket. It's that simple.

    Give Them The Goods

  4. Show Real Examples & Live Demos

    Nothing‚Äîand I mean nothing‚Äîbeats watching someone do the thing. Show before/afters. Do live demos. Walk through real examples. Abstract advice is forgettable. Watching you build an AI workflow, break down a viral post, or coach someone live? That sticks. Students learn by watching, not just listening. Be the YouTube tutorial they actually want to watch.

    Show, Don't Just Tell

  5. Make Space for Q&A, Hot Seats & Personalized Coaching

    Here's a secret: when one person gets coached, everyone learns. Hot seats are like group therapy for learning‚Äîpeople see themselves in other students' questions. They spot patterns. They think, "Oh wait, I do that too!" Q&A isn't filler time at the end. It's where lightbulbs go off.

    Where Magic Happens

  6. Create Emotional Safety Through Authenticity

    Students can smell fake from a mile away. Share your struggles. Show vulnerability. Admit when something was hard for you. When you create a safe space, students go deeper. They take bigger risks. They share more honestly. Your authenticity gives them permission to be real, too. And that's where transformation happens.

    Be Human

  7. Facilitate Community & Collective Energy

    Lonely learning sucks. Period. Students want to see other humans on the journey with them. Use breakouts. Create shares. Celebrate wins together. When students feel part of a tribe, they stay engaged, they show up, and they actually finish what they started. Community is the accountability partner they didn't know they needed.

    Nobody Grows Alone

  8. Focus on Identity Shifts, Not Just Skills

    Anyone can teach tactics. The really good stuff? That's when you help someone see themselves differently. "I'm not just learning to create content‚ÄîI'm becoming a creator." "I'm not just learning manifestation‚ÄîI'm becoming someone who trusts their power." That's the transformation students will remember five years from now.

    Go Deeper

  9. Integrate Spiritual & Philosophical Depth

    This is Mindvalley, baby. Students are here for meaning, not just money. Don't be afraid to go beyond tactics into "why it matters" territory. Connect to purpose. Talk about consciousness. Make it philosophical. The students who show up here want depth, not just surface-level hacks. Give them both.

    The Mindvalley Difference

  10. Be a Credible Expert with Real-World Experience

    Students can tell the difference between someone who's done the thing and someone who read about it. Share your real experience‚Äîthe wins AND the faceplants. They trust practitioners, not theorists. Your street cred matters. If you've built it, launched it, healed from it, or scaled it‚Äîsay so. Your scars are your credentials.

    Walk Your Talk

‚ö†Ô∏è The 10 Biggest Student Struggles

These issues show up in every program. Avoid them like the plague. üö´

  1. Tech & Access Issues

    #1 pain point across the board. Broken links, missing logins, scattered resources, can't-find-the-replay chaos. This is the fastest way to lose trust. Fix your tech, centralize your resources, test your links. It's not sexy, but it matters.

  2. Overwhelm & Content Density

    Too much content, too fast, too soon. Students love depth, but they need breathing room. More isn't always better. Sometimes the best gift you can give is space to integrate.

  3. Pacing & Long Calls

    Sessions that feel like marathons kill engagement. Tight segments, clear transitions, and respect for time make all the difference. If your call goes over, people feel guilty for leaving. Don't do that to them.

  4. Clarity Gaps & Navigation Issues

    Students shouldn't have to play detective to figure out what to do next, where to find things, or how topics connect. Make it obvious. Confusion isn't a sign of depth‚Äîit's a sign of poor design.

  5. Too Advanced for Beginners

    This one shows up in literally every Mastery. Not everyone starts where you think they start. Build clearer on-ramps. Assume less. Explain more. Beginners are brave for showing up‚Äîdon't punish them for it.

  6. Repetition Without Progression

    When you revisit a concept, students need to know why. "Advanced layer on X" works. Repeating the same idea without adding depth? That's frustrating. Make every revisit earn its keep.

  7. Missing or Delayed Resources

    You promised slides. Where are they? Even small delays break momentum and erode trust. Upload fast. Deliver what you promised. Keep your word.

  8. Not Enough Practical Examples

    Especially in advanced or conceptual lessons, students need to see it in action. Don't just explain‚Äîdemonstrate. Theory without examples is like a recipe without measurements. Technically useful, practically useless.

  9. Unequal Q&A Participation

    The same three people getting all the hot seat time? That's a problem. Rotate fairly. Track who's been coached. Everyone paid to be here. Everyone deserves a shot.

  10. Time Zones & Scheduling Pain

    Global audiences mean someone's always joining at 3am or missing the live call. Honor that reality. Make replays easy to find, keep calls reasonable in length, and acknowledge that not everyone can show up live.

üóìÔ∏è Essential Delivery Principles

The foundational practices that work across every Mastery program

Pre-Session Excellence

üì¶ Send Materials in Advance

Always send materials and links BEFORE the class. This isn't optional‚Äîit's a contractual must-have.

  • All slides, tools, templates, links, books, apps
  • Required reading or downloads
  • Special preparation instructions
  • Pre-work if needed

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical for Technical Programs: For Masteries like AI, students need time to download tools, set up accounts, and prep their environment. Email these requirements well in advance.

üéØ Define 3 Clear Learning Points

Every class should have maximum 3 main learning points. This gives students clear outcomes and helps them know what to focus on.

  • Map these at the beginning of the session
  • This mentally prepares students and directs the experience
  • When the lesson is mapped, students can relax and absorb
  • Creates a nurturing, organized environment

üîß Prepare 1-2 Actionable Tools

Every class must include practical, immediately usable tools:

  • Frameworks that simplify complex concepts
  • Checklists students can follow step-by-step
  • Templates they can customize
  • Scripts they can adapt
  • Exercises they can practice

üí° Plan Real-Life Examples

Always prepare concrete demonstrations of how to apply what you're teaching:

  • Case studies from your experience or clients
  • Hot-seat coaching to show application in real-time
  • Live demos of techniques or tools
  • Before/after transformations

Q&A Best Practices

End every session with structured Q&A following these guidelines:

‚úÖ Do This

  • Direct all questions to Zoom Q&A box (not chat)
  • Ask students to upvote questions
  • Address questions most relevant to everyone
  • Track who asks questions across sessions
  • Ensure fair rotation and diverse voices
  • Reserve adequate time (15-20 minutes minimum)

‚ùå Avoid This

  • Answering questions from chat (creates distraction)
  • Letting same students dominate every Q&A
  • Skipping Q&A because you ran over time
  • Answering overly specific questions that don't serve the group
  • Rushing through questions at the end

Homework & Implementation

Action steps should be implementable within 1 week so people actually take action.

  • Make tasks achievable in 7 days
  • Ideally, tasks build upon each other week-to-week
  • Provide clear, step-by-step instructions
  • Limit to 1-2 actions maximum per week
  • Make success measurable and specific

‚è∞ Time Management (Non-Negotiable)

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Respect Time Boundaries

People complain when calls go over time because they experience FOMO if they "miss anything."

  • Even though replays exist, it's not the same experience
  • Most students have other commitments scheduled
  • Going overtime breaks trust and creates stress
  • Build buffer time into your segments
  • End on time, every time. No exceptions.
